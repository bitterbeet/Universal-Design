Why Universal Design? (A short manifesto)

The web is — and should be — a public, participatory space. Universal design moves accessibility from a compliance checkbox to a design philosophy that benefits everyone: faster interfaces, clearer content, inclusive branding, better SEO, and fewer legal headaches.

Top arguments to bring to your team

  1. Better UX = broader market: Accessibility improves usability for all users (think: noisy trains, aging eyes, small screens).
  2. Performance & maintainability: Semantic markup and progressive enhancement reduce complexity and bugs.
  3. Ethics & equity: Design that excludes is design that harms — we make products for humans, not personas.
  4. Search & discoverability: Accessible sites are better indexed by search engines and voice assistants.
  5. Legal risk reduction: Being deliberate reduces the chance of accessibility-related litigation.

From grassroots to standards

Major shifts — CSS Zen Garden, web standards, HTML5 semantics — started as grassroots movements. Universal design belongs in that lineage. Start small (a11y-first components, color palettes with contrast tokens) and grow your practice across teams.

Pitch line

“Design once, work for many.” Use this short pitch with stakeholders — it's succinct and makes the business case plus the moral one.
